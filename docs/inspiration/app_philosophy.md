Teaching as Expanding a Worldview
A Teacher and his Pupil by Claude LefebvreGrowing up, my sister was obsessed with Pride and Prejudice. Being the male juvenile delinquent I was, I put off any interest and made a point of staying away from anything regency-era related. One day when I was indirectly teasing her about it, she made the comment that the book has already been vetted by millions of people and after 200 years the overwhelming consensus of it being good is so strong that it can be taken as an objective fact. Hence, I can't judge the book, the book judges me. Realizing that I actually didn't understand anything that was going on in the book, I pushed myself to read it, partly so I could prove her wrong. But after a few attempts, I reached a point where I could understand it, and realized it is a very goo book, and well worth reading.
This anecdote is one of my more pure teaching moments. Abstractly, it could be said that there is a large space of things in the world, and those things all have an intrinsic hierarchy of value. Everyone's world view only covers a small subset of this hierarchy. The limits of this world view are defined by what things people are able to understand enough to access and integrate into their lives. Just like how Pride and Prejudice was beyond the limits of what I could appreciate (ironically because of price), everyone has many valuable things they could enjoy but don't because it is outside of their world view. To access those better things, people's world view has to expand. This expanding is definitionally what learning is. Teaching then is nothing more or less than helping others expand their world view so that they can enjoy what is more maximally good for them.
The Min Max Optimization Problem Of Learning
How easily something can be learned is proportional to how familiar it already is. Hence, there is sometimes a tension between quickly learning what is easy and comes naturally versus slowing down and trying something that can be very good but may be more outside of limits of what is easy for them. The min max optimization of teaching then is finding finding the balance by picking something that is as valuable as possible, while still not being prohibitively difficult to learn. Solving this balance is a mix of teaching students how to aim for the best possible thing that given their situation they are able to, and finding ways to make learning that thing as easy as possible.
We will start by focusing on minimizing difficulty learning with an example, how to make memorizing the order of a deck of cards very easy.
Making Memorizing a Deck of Cards Very Easy
Memorizing a deck is hard because the order is meaninglessly random. Yet people without an inherently talented memory (myself included) can, with some training, do it in under five minutes.
To do this, one must first understand that a memory is not stored in one place. It is a pattern, a network of neurons that have learned to fire together. Its strength is structural, like a spider web: the more pathways connecting to more things, the stronger and more durable it is. A single anchor restrung for hours is far weaker than breadth of connection. So re-verbalizing a card's name over and over builds a poor memory, while connecting it to as many things as possible builds a strong one. With cards this starts by dreaming up a scene, rich in color, texture, sound, and smell, and then a story full of lore and emotion.
As an example, suppose you're trying to memorize a deck that starts with the three of clubs. For me, three reminds me of a three legged stool, and clubs feels kind of rural. Working off that, what you can imagine is a three legged stool an old 18th century Irish potato farmer uses to harvest his fields. He has to use it because his knees are giving out, and the old family stool is all he can use. Like his joints, the joints in the stool are worn down and are getting wobbly. There is dirt caked into the grain of the wood which you physically feel all the bumps and grime over. The smell is of sweat and manure. The look in the man's eye is a mix of empty vacancy in his task, with a little pain from the work and also a sad resignation at the state of his life (which can represent the two of spades), since his wife (queen of hearts) and child (jack of clubs) had died during a very ugly and painful birth (9 of diamonds) which looked exactly like this…
The reason why we are making this idea so visceral is because we're subconsciously connecting these cards to many different things at the same time: smell, color, emotion, and so on.
In addition to adding lots of details, this little story is introducing structure. Our story encodes five cards. At the top of the structure is the farmer on his stool in a field, this simple fuzzy picture represents the central starting node of our structure. Branching from it are the stool and the man. The stool's key detail, three legged and rural, is the leaf node standing for the three of clubs. Details like grain, color, and paint extend that node to more things that act as extra anchors that make the important memory of a 3 legged stool stick. Its best connection is the worn joints, tied to the man's bad joints. The man stands for the two of spades, encoded in his two eyes and their wash of sharp spade like emotion, and that emotion is a strong enough anchor to bridge to the next symbol, his wife, and on from there. Together, this structure forms a mental graph. Done across a full deck of cards, recalling the full deck doesn't require recalling more than one to a few things at a time. This is very important because our cognitive load capacity only allows us to remember about 4 things at any given time. All you have to do recall the full deck then is start at the central node, recall the 2 to 4 branches associated with, and descend down the branches to find the specific cards you're trying to memorize.
Reducing The Difficulty Of Learning
The same hierarchical indexing that makes memorizing a deck quick and easy works on almost anything, with one catch. With cards any vivid anchor works, since the cards mean nothing on their own. A real subject's anchors can't be entirely arbitrarily random: they have to at least at the top level align with reality in meaningful ways (possibly with some random richer details at the edges to add anchorage). As such, it's index is built from the subject's own structure rather than invented on the spot. Most books are written to be loaded this way, their arguments arranged into a hierarchy, and a student who absorbs that structure rather than the raw words retains far more. Science is similar, its theorems built from a few core ideas. Even physical skills run on the same index: a trained athlete conditions specific situations to drop straight down the hierarchy to the few things that matter for the next move instead of weighing everything at once.
Further, when learning about something that actually matters, we can improve on the deck technique by having the central node be the goal the learning is serving. Rooting the index at the goal does double duty: every branch beneath it is elevated by how relevant it is to the central goal, and the student gets a metric for what is worth attending to, since anything that does not contribute to the central goal can be set aside. That metric of value defines a hierarchy of what is most important to remember, and it will nudge the student to surface the most critical points as the more central nodes, and let the more cursory details fall to the periphery. To see what this looks like, we will design a lesson for one concrete skill: adding fractions.
Teaching Adding Fractions
When teaching fractions, the starting central node represents that we first introduce must represent goal of what adding fractions is all about. In a video game, we have the central node be a kitchen where the student helps mom cook. This works because most kids already have some experience helping in the kitchen, and using fractional measuring cups is a big part of cooking. The kitchen is where the student brings together what they know, shows it to mom, sees their progress, and runs into the limits that keep them from finishing a recipe. Frustration is what someone feels when they understand a goal enough to want it but cannot yet reach it. Building that feeling is the point: it plants the goal in the student's mind, and the tension gives momentum for the duller drilling to come.
The central node represents the goal of adding fractions. Ideally, this should be the abstract idea that fraction adding is the tool to do addition on partial numbers. However, going directly to this central goal and expecting a student to frame everything around it is probably too far outside of the context abilities. Instead, if we're building a video game, we can have central reason for learning fractions to be able to help mom with her cooking, a goal that most children can come in eager to want to achieve, and has a direct connection to adding fractions since adding fractions is a big part of measuring out ingredient. Hence, if we're building a video game, the central goal node or starting place for the game would be the kitchen where the student tries to help mom with her cooking.
To make the representation of this goal, the kitchen is where the student comes to do everything they can to directly help mom with her cooking, and demonstrate their progress towards their goal of better being able to help mom.
The first exercise they will do with mom in the kitchen will be something the student almost certainly knows something about already: stacking blocks to match a height. Mom asks for some amount of an ingredient, shown as a target line on a measuring stick labeled in fraction form, and the student stacks fraction blocks until they reach the line. To measure 3/4 of a cup of sugar, the student takes a pile of 1/4 cubes, stacks three up to the line, and gets a little reward. This requires no real math; it is pure matching by eye. Even though it teaches nothing new, it exposes the goal: fraction adding is what completes a measurement.
Once matching a height is comfortable, the kitchen raises the bar. Mom hands over two piles of the same kind of block and asks for the combined total in fraction form before stacking anything. Matching by eye no longer works, because the question shifted from "fill to the line" to "predict the line." The student can build a measurement but cannot yet compute one, and that frustration is the cue to teach the first core idea: adding fractions that already share a denominator. The student is pulled out of the kitchen and into the first room connected to the kitchen.
This first room represents the first core concept about adding fractions: adding fractions of the same denominator together. This is taught by having the child add unit blocks of a certain denominator of the same size into a total. The game does not stick to kitchen-realistic amounts like halves and quarters; it deliberately reaches for less natural ones like fifths and sevenths, because an unfamiliar base forces real addition rather than recall of a total already seen. This is where the matching instinct becomes real addition: the student stops stacking to find the answer and starts naming it, then checking. Mastery is not eventually getting it right but getting there quickly and directly, naming 2/7 plus 3/7 as 5/7 at a glance instead of counting pieces. Once that is automatic, they return to the kitchen, show mom, and prove to themselves and mom that they are now doing better than before.
After master is proven, they get a new recipe that pushes past the skill: one that adds fractions of unlike bases.
That recipe frustrates fast, because the pieces no longer line up. Once the frustration is shown, they get sent to a second room that represents how to multiply the top and bottom of fractions so that they have the same denominator. Once both sit on the same base, the student adds them as in the first room. Mastery is again speed and directness, reaching for the right base and multipliers without hunting. Then they return to the kitchen with a new tool for turning unlike bases into like ones. They demonstrate this skill on the exact same problems they were getting stumped on before, demonstrating to themselves and "mom" that they are progressing.
After that, recipe that if solved using the simple scaling method results in messy fractions like 33/99 or 7/14. That mess starts getting penalized, and summons the third room: simplifying fractions. Its sub concept tree feeds that core skill, first finding the factors of a single number, then the factors a numerator and denominator share, and finally dividing both by the largest shared factor, which collapses an answer to its fewest, largest pieces. Once that is fast, the student clears the messy recipes to mom who hits the kid with new questions that triggers the last wall: an answer larger than a whole cup, like 9/7, which mom cannot pour as nine-sevenths of a cup. That sends them to the final room, where they turn an improper fraction into a mixed number by dividing the numerator by the denominator to find how many whole cups it makes and how much is left, reading 9/7 as one cup and 2/7 more.
Every core room is unlocked the same way: the kitchen throws a problem the current tools cannot handle cleanly, that shortfall picks out the one room that fixes it, and the new skill is carried back and demonstrated. By the last room, they cover every step of adding any two fractions: the sense of a fraction as parts of a whole that the blocks built in the first measurement, making unlike bases match, adding across a shared base, reducing the result, and writing an over-one answer as a mixed number. Nothing in the procedure is left without a room.
Like with counting cards, the central node of why the student is learning fractions (to cook with mom) is serves as the hub of the main core skills for how to solve a fraction. Each of these skills (though not as directly expressed) also has other sub skills attached to them like how to recognize what digits are divisible by different numbers, how to translate the block representation to the numeric representation for a specific skill, and so on.
Once that structure is solid, the scaffolding comes off. The recipes return with fewer blocks and shorter measuring sticks, the stacks giving way to the bare numbers they stood for, until the child adds fractions on paper with nothing holding it up but the index they built by walking the rooms. This is the same move the memorizer makes when they finally recall a whole deck without walking their palace: the structure has moved out of the game and into the student's head. Only from there does it make sense to extend it into subtracting, multiplying, and dividing fractions.
Why This Design Work
Goal Orientation
In line with my basic theory that education is just expanding one's world view, I think this app does a few smart things.
Underlying the whole experience is a goal made explicit from the start: we are adding fractions to measure things out. It can be understood with no grasp of the math behind it; kids get it just by watching a parent pour measured ingredients into a bowl and enjoying the sweet treats that come out of that process. Having a goal is essential to doing anything at all. A goal does four things:
It gives an identity that defines what all your choices in an activity should be set to maximize.
It gives a precise metric of how effectively you are progressing.
It gives a way to determine what is meaningful for an activity.
It gives a way to "solve" all other decisions by simply determining what choice most optimizes one towards their purpose.

Someone without a clearly understood goal has nothing to direct their energy toward and wanders aimlessly, giving up the moment anything gets hard. So even for small children, a goal has to be expressed, directly or indirectly, in every activity, or nothing will hold their attention.
Children especially have limits on how far outside their normal experience they can imagine a goal. We mitigate that by building the larger goal off something already within reach, here helping mom in the kitchen. But this starting point is not what we are truly optimizing for. The goal was always full mastery of adding fractions. The cooking wrapper is a mildly helpful jump pad that fades as the child gains enough context to see, more abstractly, why mastering fractions is a truly great prize that ultimately supersedes the mere goal of being able to use measuring cups for cooking.
The same goal that orients the child also paces the lesson. Because every step is judged by whether it moves them closer to adding fractions, there is no fixed quota of questions per concept; a concept gets broken into a room only once a specific gap is actually constraining progress, and once the child clears the problem that stumped them, they return to the goal. The unlike-base recipe is the clean example: the room opens because that exact recipe is impossible without it, and handing the recipe back proves the gap is closed. Holding a child back only when a real constraint appears is what makes the app feel like it is genuinely optimizing to reach the goal as directly as it can, not filling out a checklist of things that should somehow only indirectly help.
Taking the Skills and Knowledge They Already Have As Far As They Will Go
At the start we assume a child can do basically two things: stack blocks and add numbers. With just those, we can do some crude fraction addition. As much as possible we start inside the child's world view rather than reaching past it, which primes what may have been fringe knowledge to serve as anchors for the new information. The more anchors to existing knowledge, the easier the learning sticks.
The goal is to push that existing knowledge to its limit, until it clearly falls short of the goal we set. The point of pushing that far is to expose the exact weakness, not to wear the child down. Once the gap is in full view, the child feels the conflict between the goal they want and what they cannot yet do, and the lesson pivots from pushing to drilling that one missing skill. Because the skill sits right at the edge of their understanding, the gap is as small as it can be, though closing it still takes deliberate drilling to make the skill fast and automatic.
Where the Motivation Comes From
Motivating the child and diagnosing what they are missing look like two jobs. They are one. A weakness, by itself, is inert; a child simply told they are bad at something tends to shut down. What is not inert is proving three things at once: that the weakness is real, that it is specifically what stands between the child and their goal, and that clearing it opens up much more. That bundle is the motivation.
The design proves all three concretely. The wall proves "this is holding you back": the kitchen hands the child a recipe they genuinely cannot do, so the gap is something they hit rather than something they are told about. The return proves "clearing it unblocks progress": the once-impossible recipe is now solvable, so the payoff is felt rather than promised, and the new skill is cashed out immediately as visible progress on the goal rather than an abstract checkmark. This puts the child on the same side as the app, aiming their own effort at the missing skill rather than resisting it as busywork. Neither half alone does it: a wall with no payoff is just discouragement, a payoff with no felt wall is just busywork. That is why the weakness is pushed all the way into the open: a half-exposed one convinces no one, so the diagnostic push and the motivational push are the same push. Progress toward a well-chosen goal makes that visible payoff a fair stand-in for value, which sharpens the child's sense of how to advance toward any goal, not just this one. A child who repeatedly feels which steps actually move them forward to something that matters can start to run the method themselves: setting their own goals and breaking problems into solvable pieces the way the lesson has been doing for them all along.
Not Falling for the Myth of Intrinsic Interest
The last thing worth calling out is what this design deliberately refuses to do: believe in the idea of intrinsic interest.
Built into the idea of intrinsic interest is the assumption that some things are inherently more valuable or meaningful to a particular person because of their personality. We reject that, for the same reason that runs through this whole essay and my world view: the value of a thing is objective. Its worth does not change with perception, ignorance, or bias. What changes is the cost of accessing it, which is roughly how far the thing sits from someone's limited world view.
Treating that relativism as part of the very definition of intrinsic interest may be a little unfair to the term, but if accepted as such, the idea becomes incompatible with a world view built on rigid, non-relativistic truths. In its place is something simpler: all values are and absolute, the only differentiating factor in how much people enjoy them is just what values are in their world view, which is defined by their experience, biases, knowledge, and limited understanding and accessibility to the full hierarchy of values.
As people grow and specialize, there does come a point where a specialization is strong enough that the cost of developing further in the area of specialization is so low that it makes more sense to stay in that area rather than start on something that is objectively better, but takes too much effort to learn.
However, several things can delay when that switch from focusing on the objectively best thing to specialization happens. For one, a wider world view has long term benefits, so if one is young and has a lot of life ahead of them, the short term costs of learning something new start to matter less. Further, education itself can be optimized far past what traditional teaching offers, which lowers that cost directly.
None of this though makes it the teacher's job to force a student to learn whatever he/she thinks is best for a student. If something really is objectively good, then it should be possible to convince anyone of the objective value something has, independent of people's own situations or biases. As such, the teacher's real and by far most important job is to teach one meta-skill: how to find what things have the most objective value, and aim for those things. If this is taught well, then there will hopefully come a point where the student can be given the trust to direct their learning not just to what they "want", but what is objectively the most important thing for them to learn. That is the point where a teacher can stop controlling or directing, and only be there to assist students in their drive for the truly best things.
Working towards that point, what the teacher has to do is the next best thing: convince the student that the marginal step in the right direction really is what they want, then provide the learning tools so that they can take that step. If this convincing can't be done, then that is a good signal that either the teacher picked something that actually isn't defensibly objectively good, or the student is currently incapable of appreciating something that is objectively best for them. In either case, the pivot is to find something else that is more defensible, or that the student has the foundation to be able to appreciate and be convinced of its merit.
If this teaching is done well, this should feel about the same as truly self directed learning, but with just enough extra guidance inserted so that the child will naturally choose the right direction.
My proposed fraction app does that (at least partially) by starting from something already inside the child's world view: cooking. Learning how to use fractions is objectively a good thing, and it is a good thing that students can more easily appreciate how fractions help with that. However, the goal of learning that is subservient to the real, objectively more important goal of teaching fractions. As students grow their cooking skills by learning fractions, they are directly facing the constraints that understanding fractions solve, and overcoming those constraints gives them enough of a foundation to eventually be convinced that mastering fractions really is worthwhile. Once that convincing is finally done, the teacher can step back and let that conviction motivate the student to direct their learning in getting through the rest of the learning material.
Focusing On The Highest Goals
Earlier in the paper, I said that the education problem is minimizing the difficulty in learning, and maximizing goals towards the best things that students can aim for. Hopefully from the last section, it should be clear that these objectives aren't mutually exclusive. Education is truly easier when students are focused on learning the things that matter the most, and they know it.
A full discussion of what it means to aim for the highest possible thing is in my other essay On Joy And The Purpose Of Life so I won't go deep into this subject. Suffice to say though, probably one of the most fundamental issues in the human experience, or really even life itself, is how everything slowly devolves to chaos. When someone tries to focus on one thing, it doesn't take long before other needs become overwhelmingly loud and demand attention before anything can be completed. At the same time, developing anything that might mitigate this requires taking on more chaos from the uncertainty, resource expenditure, and effort that is required to develop anything that is outside of one's world view. Teaching people how to break the cycle and find stability by aiming for higher better things and proving a practical way to actually move towards those things is the fundamental goal of education. Doing this right traditionally has been measured by evaluating how many of the basic core life concepts people have mastered. However, the one step above this is teaching the meta skill of how to find something to aim for, and how to progress towards that. The secret to doing this well is equivalent to the solution of the min max problem introduced at the start of the paper: teach them how to love what is objectively good, even if they can't fully understand it, how to use whatever skills are available to them to move towards that, and that developing yourself further by expanding your world view is always (at least in the long term) very much so worth the cost.
